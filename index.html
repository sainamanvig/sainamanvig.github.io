01 — About

Curious by
disposition.

I was born in Panipat, India, and attended Penn State University Park from Fall 2025. Prior to Penn State, I spent years developing projects in my own room: an innovative biometric attendance system, a sensor glove that won several awards, and some useless small projects that I found fascinating enough to develop anyway. The habit of developing interesting problems myself without waiting for anyone to allow me to do that formed everything about who I am today.

Currently, I'm majoring in Computer Science with a special focus on its mathematical aspects such as formal logic, computational geometry, and information mathematics. Additionally, I have been serving as a Learning Assistant for Calculus II during my time at Penn State, teaching me that explaining something is probably the toughest challenge to comprehending the concept.

By putting all those experiences together, this is an attempt to create a portfolio representing the connection between different sides of myself – my passion for working with intriguing problems and my love for rigorous thinking.

In the future, I plan to continue my education and eventually get a job at a research university – not for security purposes but simply because I would like to work at a research university

02 — Theme & Rhetoric

The work collected here grows from a single conviction: that thinking carefully and building honestly are the same activity, differently dressed.

The artifacts within this portfolio include hardware and theoretical work, professional practice in industry and academia, and individual creation as well as collaborative work in public communities. What ties the collection together is the orientation, the drive to understand the world through knowledge rather than taking it for granted.

I selected each of these artifacts based on their role in documenting a point when I encountered some kind of difficulty with a specific project and pushed through it until I created something. This persistence is the message of the portfolio: that there is a direction and it is one which requires work to achieve.

While the primary intended audience for this portfolio will be a writing instructor, my colleagues in class will also serve as part of the audience, even if they do not have a background in computer science. In this case, I hope to provide enough context around each artifact regarding its problem, process, and meaning without being overly technical. The secondary audience of peers and potential collaborators will receive the message of the portfolio in the work itself.

I

Rigor

The belief that understanding something means being able to say precisely what it is — and precisely what it is not. This shows up in a published paper, in the careful annotation of training data, and in helping a student understand why a proof works rather than just that it does.

II

Experimentation

The willingness to try something before knowing whether it will work. Hardware is particularly unforgiving: the glove either registers motion or it does not. That discipline has trained a tolerance for failure and a preference for small, concrete tests over large, abstract plans.

III

Contribution

The conviction that good work is meant to be shared. Open-source contributions, a published paper, a utility for Penn State's student community — each extends what I know into something others can use, critique, and improve. This is the form my ambition tends to take.

01
Research · Mathematics

Segment Space: Vector-like Entities with Positional Context

In early 2025, I wrote and independently published a short mathematical paper on Zenodo — an open-access repository used by researchers worldwide. The paper proposes a new way of thinking about line segments: rather than treating them as static geometric objects, it frames them as vector-like entities that carry both direction and position. The construction sits at the intersection of geometry and linear algebra, and it emerged from a question I could not stop asking. Writing it forced me to do something harder than finding an idea: to articulate it as formal argument, to say exactly what I meant and nothing more. This is the piece in this portfolio I am most attached to, because it is the purest record of how I think when no one is assigning the problem.

Published on Zenodo — zenodo.org/records/15355799
02
Software · Privacy

TG-Chan: An Anonymous Sub-Social Network

TG-Chan is a social platform I built in Python that uses cryptographic hashing to anonymize user posts while still allowing interaction. The project began as a genuine question: can a digital space be designed so that ideas are weighed on their own merits, free from the pressures of reputation, appearance, and status? The answer the software gives is provisional and imperfect — all architectures for human communication are — but the attempt was instructive. Privacy, I learned, is an engineering decision before it is a political one. Every structural choice about how data is stored, transmitted, and displayed encodes an assumption about what communication ought to be. TG-Chan is the artifact in this collection that most directly reflects my values.

Python · Cryptographic Hashing · 2024
03
Civic Tech · Web

PSCIndex: A Penn State Community Index

Arriving at a large university as an international student is disorienting in ways that are difficult to explain. The community of student organizations is vast and, for a newcomer, opaque. PSCIndex is a lightweight web application I built to make that landscape navigable: it indexes Penn State student communities with full-text search, category filtering, tagging, and sharing capabilities. The project is not technically complex, and I did not mean it to be. It is here because of what it represents — a decision to spend time making something genuinely useful for people I had never met, without a course requirement or a prize to motivate it. I think the most honest test of whether a project deserved to exist is whether someone other than its creator is glad it does.

JavaScript · TailwindCSS · 2025
04
Hardware · Embedded Systems

GyroHand Controller

The GyroHand is a modular, Arduino-based glove that translates the motion of a hand into precise digital control signals. It was designed for adaptability: each sensor element can be swapped, adjusted, or reprogrammed independently. At the state-level Science Conclave in India, it was awarded second prize. The project required solving mechanical, electrical, and software problems in parallel, and those problems did not yield to each other graciously. Hardware, I discovered, does not forgive the way software does: you cannot comment out a broken circuit. That experience — of working in a medium that is indifferent to good intentions — permanently changed how I think about designing and building things.

Arduino · C · 2nd Prize, State Science Conclave
05
Professional · Machine Learning

ML Internship — Videolytical Systems

From January to September 2024, I worked as a machine learning intern at Videolytical Systems Pvt. Ltd., a computer vision company, where I was part of the training data management team. My work was infrastructural: preprocessing images, managing annotation pipelines, and performing quality assurance on large-scale datasets. The experience was humbling in a useful way. Machine learning as practiced in industry is, at the data layer, an exercise in patience, precision, and organizational discipline — qualities that matter far more than algorithmic elegance when the dataset has millions of entries. I came away with a clear respect for the unglamorous work that makes intelligent systems possible, and a sharper sense of where the interesting research problems actually live.

Python · Pandas · Computer Vision · Jan – Sep 2024
06
Teaching · Mathematics

Learning Assistant, Calculus II

Since January 2026, I have worked as a Learning Assistant for MATH 141 — Calculus II — at Penn State, under Dr. Dylan Attwell-Duval. The role involves facilitating collaborative problem-solving sessions and helping students through the conceptual difficulties that arise when moving from computation to rigorous mathematical reasoning. I include it here not as a credential but as a reflection. Teaching, I have found, is a form of intellectual self-examination: to explain something well, you must first discover where your own understanding is incomplete. Every question a student asks is an invitation to be more precise. This is the most demanding feedback loop I have encountered, and the most rewarding.

Penn State MATH 141 · Jan 2026 – Present
07
Open Source · Community

Open Source Contributions (200+ Projects)

Over several years, I have contributed code to more than two hundred open-source projects, including LibreTube, BlissOS, and SuperImage. Open source is the closest analogue software has to a public commons: code built in the open, reviewed by strangers, improved over time, and freely available to anyone who needs it. Contributing to it is, I think, a form of civic participation — a way of giving something back to the infrastructure that made it possible to learn in the first place. It has also been an education in itself. Reading large, unfamiliar codebases; understanding why decisions were made years before you arrived; writing code that other people must actually be able to read and modify — these are skills that no single project of your own can teach you.

04 — Reflection

Growth, Commitment,
and What Comes Next

Taking all seven artifacts into consideration together, I find something that I did not recognize until creating each one of them on its own, that all of these pieces try, in some way, to bridge the gap between a concept and its implications. With the mathematical paper, the question posed is what would happen if one were to consider the directedness and positioning properties of a line segment – a theoretical concept with the possibility of practical implications in computing geometrical figures. With the anonymous social network, the question asked is what communications would be like if all societal pressures were stripped away from it. With the community index, the question raised is what navigating in a strange area would feel like.

The most honest test of whether a project deserved to exist is whether someone other than its creator is glad it does.

The most substantial change I believe that I've undergone has been in how I engage with challenges. Early works were scrapped once they ceased to be effortless. Those later works, the glove, the internship, the paper, entailed staying with true confusions for long stretches. The Learning Assistantship has taken that even farther, where, in a classroom, deferring confusion is impossible. There's always a student who's waiting for your answer, and whether you can convey what you know will prove the adequacy of your knowledge. In this way, I've come to be more of an honest thinker.

The ideals reflected in this portfolio, those of rigor, exploration, and service, are not endpoints; rather, they are the foundation stones of an intellectual character constructed in real-time based upon the objects that I've elected to pursue. I'm nineteen years old. I've authored one article, developed several projects, made several hundred contributions to repositories, and, through a class at Penn State, discovered the difference between knowing something and delivering that knowledge. Both are vital. I'll be doing that for a while yet to come.

The trajectory I am on points toward graduate study in theoretical computer science or computational mathematics, and eventually toward a research university, where I hope to do the work of thinking at the frontier and help others learn to do the same. This portfolio is not an application. It is a record of how I got here, and a statement of where I am going.
